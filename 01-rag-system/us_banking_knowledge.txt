SECTION 1: BANK SECRECY ACT (BSA)
================================================================

OVERVIEW
The Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), enacted in 1970, is the primary U.S. anti-money laundering law.
It requires financial institutions to assist U.S. government agencies in detecting and preventing
money laundering and other financial crimes. The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN),
a bureau of the U.S. Department of the Treasury, administers the BSA.

KEY BSA REQUIREMENTS
1. Customer Identification Program (CIP)
   - Banks must verify the identity of every customer opening an account
   - Required information: name, date of birth, address, identification number (SSN/EIN)
   - Must verify identity through documentary or non-documentary methods
   - Must check customers against government lists of known terrorists and criminals

2. Customer Due Diligence (CDD)
   - Banks must understand the nature and purpose of customer relationships
   - Must identify and verify beneficial owners of legal entity customers
   - Beneficial owner = any individual owning 25% or more of a legal entity
   - Must conduct ongoing monitoring of customer transactions

3. Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)
   - Banks must file SARs for transactions involving $5,000 or more
   - Required when bank suspects funds are from illegal activity
   - Must be filed within 30 days of detecting suspicious activity
   - SARs are confidential — banks cannot tell customers a SAR was filed

4. Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs)
   - Required for cash transactions exceeding $10,000 in a single day
   - Includes multiple transactions that together exceed $10,000
   - Must be filed within 15 days of the transaction
   - Applies to deposits, withdrawals, exchanges, and other payments

5. Recordkeeping Requirements
   - Banks must retain records for minimum 5 years
   - Includes records of cash purchases, wire transfers, and loans
   - Wire transfers over $3,000 must include full sender and recipient information

SECTION 2: ANTI-MONEY LAUNDERING (AML) — U.S. FRAMEWORK
================================================================

OVERVIEW
Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained money appear legitimate.
U.S. AML regulations are enforced by FinCEN, FDIC, OCC, Federal Reserve, and SEC.

THREE STAGES OF MONEY LAUNDERING
1. Placement — Introducing illegal cash into the financial system
2. Layering — Concealing the trail through complex transactions
3. Integration — Making the money appear legitimate and usable

U.S. AML PROGRAM REQUIREMENTS
All U.S. banks must have a written AML program that includes:
- Internal policies, procedures, and controls
- Designation of a BSA/AML Compliance Officer
- Ongoing employee training program
- Independent testing and auditing
- Risk-based customer due diligence

AML RED FLAGS (U.S. SPECIFIC)
- Large cash deposits inconsistent with customer's business
- Frequent wire transfers to high-risk jurisdictions
- Customers who avoid reporting thresholds (structuring)
- Rapid movement of funds through multiple accounts
- Transactions with no apparent business purpose
- Use of shell companies or nominee accounts
- Sudden large changes in transaction patterns

STRUCTURING (ILLEGAL)
Structuring is breaking up transactions to avoid the $10,000 CTR threshold.
Example: Making $9,500 deposits on multiple days to avoid reporting.
Structuring is a federal crime under 31 U.S.C. § 5324.
Penalties include up to 5 years imprisonment and asset forfeiture.

OFAC COMPLIANCE
The Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) administers U.S. economic sanctions.
Banks must screen all transactions against OFAC's Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list.
Transactions with sanctioned individuals or countries must be blocked and reported.
Violations can result in civil penalties up to $1 million per violation.

SECTION 3: KNOW YOUR CUSTOMER (KYC) — U.S. STANDARDS
================================================================

OVERVIEW
KYC is the process by which banks verify the identity of their clients.
U.S. KYC requirements are governed by FinCEN's CDD Rule (effective May 2018).

KYC FOUR PILLARS
1. Customer Identification and Verification
   - Collect: legal name, date of birth, address, ID number
   - Verify: government-issued ID, passport, driver's license
   - Enhanced verification for high-risk customers

2. Customer Due Diligence (CDD)
   - Understand customer's business and expected transactions
   - Assess risk level: low, medium, high
   - Low risk: basic CDD sufficient
   - High risk: Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) required

3. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)
   - Required for Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)
   - Required for customers from high-risk countries
   - Required for correspondent banking relationships
   - Includes source of funds verification
   - Senior management approval often required

4. Ongoing Monitoring
   - Continuously monitor transactions against expected behavior
   - Update customer information regularly
   - Re-verify identity when significant changes occur
   - File SARs when suspicious activity detected

BENEFICIAL OWNERSHIP RULE
Effective May 2018, banks must identify beneficial owners of legal entity customers.
- Must identify all individuals owning 25% or more
- Must identify one individual with significant management control
- Beneficial owners must provide: name, DOB, address, ID number
- Banks must verify this information

SECTION 4: FDIC — FEDERAL DEPOSIT INSURANCE CORPORATION
================================================================

OVERVIEW
The FDIC is an independent U.S. government agency created in 1933.
It insures deposits at U.S. banks and savings institutions.
The FDIC supervises state-chartered banks that are not members of the Federal Reserve System.

DEPOSIT INSURANCE
- Standard insurance: $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per account category
- Account categories: single accounts, joint accounts, retirement accounts, trust accounts
- Depositors do not need to apply — insurance is automatic
- Over 99% of all U.S. banks are FDIC insured

FDIC BANK EXAMINATION
FDIC examines banks using the CAMELS rating system:
- C — Capital adequacy
- A — Asset quality
- M — Management
- E — Earnings
- L — Liquidity
- S — Sensitivity to market risk

Each component rated 1 (best) to 5 (worst). Composite rating determines supervisory action.

FDIC CONSUMER PROTECTION REGULATIONS
- Truth in Savings Act: Banks must clearly disclose interest rates and fees
- Equal Credit Opportunity Act: Prohibits discrimination in lending
- Fair Housing Act: Prohibits discrimination in mortgage lending
- Community Reinvestment Act (CRA): Banks must serve all community segments

SECTION 5: FEDERAL RESERVE REGULATIONS
================================================================

OVERVIEW
The Federal Reserve (Fed) is the central bank of the United States.
It supervises bank holding companies and state-chartered member banks.
The Fed sets monetary policy and regulates the U.S. payment system.

KEY FEDERAL RESERVE REGULATIONS
Regulation B — Equal Credit Opportunity
- Prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, national origin, sex, age
- Requires creditors to notify applicants of credit decisions within 30 days
- Must provide specific reasons for adverse credit actions

Regulation C — Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA)
- Requires banks to collect and report mortgage lending data
- Data used to identify discriminatory lending patterns
- Applies to banks making 25+ home purchase loans per year

Regulation D — Reserve Requirements
- Sets reserve requirements for depository institutions
- Banks must maintain reserves as percentage of certain deposits
- Reserve requirements currently set at 0% (changed in 2020)

Regulation E — Electronic Fund Transfers
- Protects consumers using electronic banking services
- Limits consumer liability for unauthorized transfers
- Unauthorized transfer reported within 2 days: max liability $50
- Reported within 60 days: max liability $500
- After 60 days: unlimited liability

Regulation Z — Truth in Lending Act (TILA)
- Requires clear disclosure of loan terms and costs
- Annual Percentage Rate (APR) must be clearly stated
- Right of rescission: consumers can cancel certain loans within 3 days
- Applies to credit cards, mortgages, auto loans, personal loans

Regulation CC — Availability of Funds
- Sets rules for how quickly banks must make deposited funds available
- Next business day: cash, wire transfers, government checks
- Second business day: local checks
- Extended holds allowed for large deposits, new accounts, repeated overdrafts

SECTION 6: OCC — OFFICE OF THE COMPTROLLER OF THE CURRENCY
================================================================

OVERVIEW
The OCC is a federal agency within the U.S. Department of Treasury.
It charters, regulates, and supervises all national banks and federal savings associations.
The OCC ensures banks operate safely, soundly, and fairly.

OCC RISK FRAMEWORK
The OCC assesses eight categories of risk:
1. Credit Risk — Risk of loan default
2. Interest Rate Risk — Risk from interest rate changes
3. Liquidity Risk — Risk of inability to meet obligations
4. Price Risk — Risk from market price changes
5. Operational Risk — Risk from failed processes or systems
6. Compliance Risk — Risk from violating laws and regulations
7. Strategic Risk — Risk from poor business decisions
8. Reputation Risk — Risk from negative public opinion

NATIONAL BANK REQUIREMENTS
- Must maintain minimum capital ratios
- Tier 1 Capital Ratio: minimum 6%
- Total Capital Ratio: minimum 8%
- Leverage Ratio: minimum 4%
- Well-capitalized banks: Tier 1 ≥ 8%, Total ≥ 10%

SECTION 7: CFPB — CONSUMER FINANCIAL PROTECTION BUREAU
================================================================

OVERVIEW
The CFPB was created by the Dodd-Frank Act in 2010.
It protects consumers in the financial marketplace.
The CFPB enforces federal consumer financial laws.

KEY CFPB REGULATIONS
Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA)
- Prohibits abusive debt collection practices
- Collectors cannot call before 8 AM or after 9 PM
- Must stop contact if consumer requests in writing
- Cannot use harassment, false statements, or unfair practices

Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)
- Regulates collection and use of consumer credit information
- Consumers have right to free annual credit report
- Must be notified if credit report used against them
- Can dispute inaccurate information — must be investigated within 30 days

Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) — Privacy Rule
- Financial institutions must explain information-sharing practices
- Must protect sensitive customer data
- Must provide opt-out options for sharing with non-affiliated third parties
- Annual privacy notices required

Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA)
- Requires disclosure of settlement costs for home purchases
- Prohibits kickbacks between settlement service providers
- Loan Estimate must be provided within 3 business days of application
- Closing Disclosure must be provided 3 business days before closing

SECTION 8: U.S. CREDIT RISK MANAGEMENT
================================================================

OVERVIEW
Credit risk is the risk that a borrower will default on their loan obligations.
U.S. banks manage credit risk through underwriting standards, portfolio monitoring, and reserves.

CREDIT SCORING (U.S. SYSTEM)
FICO Score ranges:
- 800-850: Exceptional
- 740-799: Very Good
- 670-739: Good
- 580-669: Fair
- Below 580: Poor

Key factors in FICO score:
- Payment history: 35%
- Amounts owed: 30%
- Length of credit history: 15%
- New credit: 10%
- Credit mix: 10%

LOAN CLASSIFICATION SYSTEM (OCC Standards)
- Pass: No significant credit weakness
- Special Mention: Potential weakness deserving management attention
- Substandard: Inadequately protected, well-defined weakness
- Doubtful: Full collection questionable, serious weakness
- Loss: Considered uncollectible, must be charged off

ALLOWANCE FOR LOAN AND LEASE LOSSES (ALLL)
Banks must maintain reserves for expected loan losses.
Current Expected Credit Loss (CECL) model (effective 2023):
- Banks must estimate lifetime expected losses at origination
- More forward-looking than previous incurred loss model
- Requires more data and sophisticated modeling

SECTION 9: U.S. FINTECH AND AI REGULATIONS
================================================================

OVERVIEW
The use of AI and machine learning in U.S. banking is growing rapidly.
Regulators have issued guidance on responsible AI use in financial services.

KEY AI REGULATORY GUIDANCE
FDIC, OCC, Fed, CFPB Joint Statement on AI (2021)
- Banks must ensure AI models are fair and unbiased
- Must be able to explain AI decisions to customers
- Must monitor AI models for performance degradation
- Must have human oversight of AI-driven decisions

Fair Lending and AI
- AI models used in credit decisions must comply with Equal Credit Opportunity Act
- Cannot use protected characteristics (race, gender, age) directly or as proxies
- Explainability required: must provide specific reasons for adverse actions
- Regular bias testing required

Model Risk Management (SR 11-7 Guidance)
- Banks must have robust model validation processes
- Three lines of defense: model developers, risk management, internal audit
- Models must be documented, validated, and monitored
- High-risk models require more rigorous validation

SECTION 10: DODD-FRANK ACT KEY PROVISIONS
================================================================

OVERVIEW
The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (2010)
was enacted in response to the 2008 financial crisis.

KEY PROVISIONS
Volcker Rule
- Prohibits banks from proprietary trading with their own accounts
- Restricts banks from owning hedge funds or private equity funds
- Applies to banks with $10 billion+ in assets

Stress Testing (DFAST)
- Banks with $100 billion+ in assets must conduct annual stress tests
- Tests assess ability to withstand severe economic scenarios
- Results published publicly
- Fed conducts its own stress tests (CCAR) for largest banks

Systemic Risk Oversight
- Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC) created
- Identifies and monitors systemic risks to U.S. financial system
- Can designate non-bank financial companies as systemically important

Derivatives Regulation
- Most derivatives must be traded on exchanges or swap execution facilities
- Central clearing required for standardized derivatives
- Reporting requirements for all derivatives transactions
