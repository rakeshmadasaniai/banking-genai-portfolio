=== SECTION 1: RBI GUIDELINES & MONETARY POLICY ===

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is India's central bank and monetary authority. It was established on April 1, 1935, under the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934.

Repo Rate: The rate at which RBI lends money to commercial banks. When repo rate increases, borrowing becomes expensive, reducing liquidity. As of 2024, the repo rate is 6.50%.

Reverse Repo Rate: The rate at which RBI borrows money from commercial banks. It is currently 3.35%.

Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR): The percentage of a bank's total deposits that must be kept as reserves with RBI. Current CRR is 4.50%. Banks earn no interest on CRR balances.

Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR): The percentage of deposits banks must maintain in liquid assets like gold, government securities. Current SLR is 18%.

Marginal Standing Facility (MSF): Rate at which banks can borrow overnight funds from RBI against government securities. MSF rate is typically 0.25% above the repo rate.

Bank Rate: The rate at which RBI lends money to commercial banks without any collateral. It is used for long-term lending.

Inflation Targeting: RBI follows flexible inflation targeting with a target of 4% CPI inflation, with a band of +/- 2%.

Open Market Operations (OMO): RBI buys or sells government securities to control money supply. Buying securities injects liquidity; selling withdraws liquidity.

Liquidity Adjustment Facility (LAF): A framework through which RBI manages short-term liquidity using repo and reverse repo operations.

Priority Sector Lending (PSL): RBI mandates that 40% of bank's net credit must go to priority sectors including agriculture, MSMEs, education, housing, and social infrastructure.

=== SECTION 2: CREDIT RISK MANAGEMENT ===

Credit risk is the risk of loss arising from a borrower's failure to repay a loan or meet contractual obligations.

Credit Risk Components:
- Probability of Default (PD): Likelihood that a borrower will default within a given time horizon, typically one year.
- Loss Given Default (LGD): The percentage of exposure that will be lost if a borrower defaults, after accounting for recovery.
- Exposure at Default (EAD): The total value a bank is exposed to at the time of default.
- Expected Loss (EL) = PD × LGD × EAD

Credit Rating Systems: Banks use internal rating systems to classify borrowers. Ratings range from AAA (highest quality) to D (default). External agencies like CRISIL, ICRA, CARE, and India Ratings provide credit ratings in India.

Non-Performing Assets (NPA): A loan is classified as NPA when interest or principal remains overdue for more than 90 days.
- Substandard Asset: NPA for less than 12 months
- Doubtful Asset: NPA for more than 12 months
- Loss Asset: Asset where loss has been identified

Provisioning Norms: Banks must maintain provisions against NPAs.
- Substandard: 15% provision
- Doubtful (up to 1 year): 25% provision
- Doubtful (1-3 years): 40% provision
- Doubtful (over 3 years): 100% provision
- Loss assets: 100% provision

Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR): Also known as CRAR. Minimum CAR under Basel III is 9% in India (RBI requirement), higher than Basel III's 8% global minimum. Formula: CAR = (Tier 1 Capital + Tier 2 Capital) / Risk Weighted Assets.

Tier 1 Capital: Core capital including common equity, retained earnings, and disclosed reserves.
Tier 2 Capital: Supplementary capital including revaluation reserves, hybrid instruments, and subordinated debt.

Stressed Assets: Include NPAs, restructured loans, and written-off assets.

=== SECTION 3: LOANS & LENDING ===

Types of Loans:
- Term Loans: Loans with fixed repayment schedule over a defined period.
- Working Capital Loans: Short-term loans to fund day-to-day operations.
- Overdraft: Credit facility allowing withdrawal beyond account balance up to a limit.
- Cash Credit: Similar to overdraft but specifically for businesses against stock/debtors.
- Letter of Credit (LC): Bank guarantee for trade transactions.
- Bank Guarantee (BG): Bank's commitment to pay if the customer defaults.

Home Loans: Secured loans against property. Typical LTV (Loan to Value) ratio is up to 80% for loans above Rs. 30 lakhs.

MCLR (Marginal Cost of Funds based Lending Rate): Benchmark rate introduced in 2016 for floating rate loans. Banks cannot lend below MCLR for most loans.

External Benchmark Lending Rate (EBLR): From October 2019, floating rate loans for retail and MSMEs must be linked to external benchmarks like RBI repo rate.

Loan Classification by Security:
- Secured Loans: Backed by collateral (home loans, auto loans, gold loans)
- Unsecured Loans: No collateral (personal loans, credit cards)

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR): Measures ability to service debt. DSCR = Net Operating Income / Total Debt Service. DSCR above 1.25 is generally acceptable.

Loan Restructuring: Process of modifying loan terms when borrower faces difficulty. Options include reducing interest rate, extending tenure, or providing moratorium.

=== SECTION 4: KYC (KNOW YOUR CUSTOMER) ===

KYC is a mandatory process for banks and financial institutions to verify customer identity and assess risk.

KYC Components:
- Customer Identification Program (CIP): Collecting and verifying customer identity documents.
- Customer Due Diligence (CDD): Assessing the nature of the customer's business and transaction patterns.
- Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD): Applied to high-risk customers such as Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs).
- Ongoing Monitoring: Continuous monitoring of customer transactions.

KYC Documents for Individuals:
- Proof of Identity: Aadhaar, PAN Card, Passport, Voter ID, Driving License
- Proof of Address: Utility bills, bank statements, Aadhaar, Passport
- Photograph

KYC Documents for Companies:
- Certificate of Incorporation
- Memorandum and Articles of Association
- Board Resolution
- List of Directors and Beneficial Owners

Beneficial Owner: A person who ultimately owns or controls more than 25% of shares or voting rights in an entity.

Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs): Individuals who hold or have held prominent public positions. Banks must apply EDD for PEPs.

Risk Categorization: Customers are classified as Low, Medium, or High risk based on occupation, transaction patterns, and geography.

KYC Periodicity:
- High-risk customers: Every 2 years
- Medium-risk customers: Every 8 years
- Low-risk customers: Every 10 years

Video-based Customer Identification Process (V-CIP): RBI-approved digital KYC method using live video interaction.

Central KYC Registry (CKYC): Centralized repository of KYC records maintained by CERSAI. One-time KYC valid across financial institutions.

=== SECTION 5: AML (ANTI-MONEY LAUNDERING) ===

Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained funds appear legitimate.

Three Stages of Money Laundering:
1. Placement: Introducing illegal funds into the financial system (e.g., cash deposits).
2. Layering: Concealing the trail through complex transactions (wire transfers, shell companies).
3. Integration: Reintroducing funds into the economy as legitimate money.

Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA) 2002: India's primary AML legislation. Requires banks to maintain records and report suspicious transactions.

Financial Intelligence Unit - India (FIU-IND): Nodal agency for receiving and analyzing suspicious transaction reports.

Reporting Requirements:
- Cash Transaction Report (CTR): All cash transactions above Rs. 10 lakhs must be reported monthly.
- Suspicious Transaction Report (STR): Any transaction suspected of money laundering must be reported within 7 days.
- Non-Profit Organization Transaction Report (NTR): Transactions by NPOs above Rs. 10 lakhs.
- Cross Border Wire Transfer Report (CWTR): All cross-border transactions above Rs. 5 lakhs.

Red Flags for Suspicious Transactions:
- Unusual large cash deposits or withdrawals
- Multiple transactions just below reporting thresholds (structuring)
- Transactions inconsistent with customer's business profile
- Rapid movement of funds through multiple accounts
- Transactions involving high-risk jurisdictions

FATF (Financial Action Task Force): International body setting global AML/CFT standards. India is a FATF member.

CFT (Combating Financing of Terrorism): Related to AML; banks must screen customers against terror watchlists like UN Security Council sanctions list.

Hawala: Informal value transfer system used to move money outside formal banking, commonly associated with money laundering.

=== SECTION 6: BANKING PRODUCTS & SERVICES ===

Current Account: Non-interest bearing account for businesses. Allows unlimited transactions.
Savings Account: Interest-bearing account for individuals. RBI deregulated savings account interest rates in 2011.
Fixed Deposit (FD): Term deposit with fixed interest rate for fixed tenure. DICGC insures deposits up to Rs. 5 lakhs.
Recurring Deposit (RD): Fixed monthly deposit for a fixed period earning interest.

NEFT (National Electronic Funds Transfer): Batch-based fund transfer system. Available 24x7 since December 2019.
RTGS (Real Time Gross Settlement): Real-time fund transfer for high-value transactions (minimum Rs. 2 lakhs). Available 24x7.
IMPS (Immediate Payment Service): Instant fund transfer available 24x7. No minimum amount.
UPI (Unified Payments Interface): Real-time payment system by NPCI. Enables instant transfers using VPA.

SWIFT: Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication. Used for international fund transfers.

Demat Account: Electronic account to hold securities. Managed by NSDL or CDSL depositories.

=== SECTION 7: BASEL NORMS ===

Basel I (1988): Introduced minimum capital requirement of 8% of risk-weighted assets. Only covered credit risk.

Basel II (2004): Three pillars framework:
1. Minimum Capital Requirements (credit, market, operational risk)
2. Supervisory Review Process
3. Market Discipline (disclosure requirements)

Basel III (2010, post-financial crisis): Strengthened capital requirements:
- Minimum Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1): 4.5%
- Tier 1 Capital: 6%
- Total Capital: 8% (9% in India)
- Capital Conservation Buffer: 2.5%
- Countercyclical Buffer: 0-2.5%
- Leverage Ratio: Minimum 3%
- Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR): Banks must hold enough high-quality liquid assets to cover 30 days of net cash outflows.
- Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR): Long-term stable funding must cover long-term assets.

=== SECTION 8: FINANCIAL RATIOS & ANALYSIS ===

Net Interest Margin (NIM): (Interest Income - Interest Expense) / Average Earning Assets. Measures profitability of lending activities.

Return on Assets (ROA): Net Income / Total Assets. Measures how efficiently a bank uses its assets. Good ROA for Indian banks: above 1%.

Return on Equity (ROE): Net Income / Shareholders' Equity. Measures return to shareholders. Good ROE: above 12-15%.

Cost to Income Ratio: Operating Expenses / Net Income. Lower is better. Good ratio: below 50%.

Gross NPA Ratio: Gross NPAs / Gross Advances. Measures asset quality. Lower is better.

Net NPA Ratio: Net NPAs / Net Advances. After provisions. Good ratio: below 2%.

PCR (Provision Coverage Ratio): Provisions / Gross NPAs. Higher means better provisioned. RBI recommends above 70%.

CASA Ratio: Current + Savings Deposits / Total Deposits. Higher CASA means lower cost of funds.

Credit Deposit (CD) Ratio: Total Loans / Total Deposits. RBI monitors this; high ratio may indicate liquidity risk.
