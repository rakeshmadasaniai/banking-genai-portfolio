=== SECTION 1: RBI GUIDELINES & MONETARY POLICY ===

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is India's central bank and monetary authority. It was established on April 1, 1935, under the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934.

Repo Rate: The rate at which RBI lends money to commercial banks. When repo rate increases, borrowing becomes expensive, reducing liquidity. As of 2024, the repo rate is 6.50%.

Reverse Repo Rate: The rate at which RBI borrows money from commercial banks. It is currently 3.35%.

Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR): The percentage of a bank's total deposits that must be kept as reserves with RBI. Current CRR is 4.50%. Banks earn no interest on CRR balances.

Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR): The percentage of deposits banks must maintain in liquid assets like gold, government securities. Current SLR is 18%.

Marginal Standing Facility (MSF): Rate at which banks can borrow overnight funds from RBI against government securities. MSF rate is typically 0.25% above the repo rate.

Bank Rate: The rate at which RBI lends money to commercial banks without any collateral. It is used for long-term lending.

Inflation Targeting: RBI follows flexible inflation targeting with a target of 4% CPI inflation, with a band of +/- 2%.

Open Market Operations (OMO): RBI buys or sells government securities to control money supply. Buying securities injects liquidity; selling withdraws liquidity.

Liquidity Adjustment Facility (LAF): A framework through which RBI manages short-term liquidity using repo and reverse repo operations.

Priority Sector Lending (PSL): RBI mandates that 40% of bank's net credit must go to priority sectors including agriculture, MSMEs, education, housing, and social infrastructure.

=== SECTION 2: CREDIT RISK MANAGEMENT ===

Credit risk is the risk of loss arising from a borrower's failure to repay a loan or meet contractual obligations.

Credit Risk Components:
- Probability of Default (PD): Likelihood that a borrower will default within a given time horizon, typically one year.
- Loss Given Default (LGD): The percentage of exposure that will be lost if a borrower defaults, after accounting for recovery.
- Exposure at Default (EAD): The total value a bank is exposed to at the time of default.
- Expected Loss (EL) = PD × LGD × EAD

Credit Rating Systems: Banks use internal rating systems to classify borrowers. Ratings range from AAA (highest quality) to D (default). External agencies like CRISIL, ICRA, CARE, and India Ratings provide credit ratings in India.

Non-Performing Assets (NPA): A loan is classified as NPA when interest or principal remains overdue for more than 90 days.
- Substandard Asset: NPA for less than 12 months
- Doubtful Asset: NPA for more than 12 months
- Loss Asset: Asset where loss has been identified

Provisioning Norms: Banks must maintain provisions against NPAs.
- Substandard: 15% provision
- Doubtful (up to 1 year): 25% provision
- Doubtful (1-3 years): 40% provision
- Doubtful (over 3 years): 100% provision
- Loss assets: 100% provision

Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR): Also known as CRAR. Minimum CAR under Basel III is 9% in India (RBI requirement), higher than Basel III's 8% global minimum. Formula: CAR = (Tier 1 Capital + Tier 2 Capital) / Risk Weighted Assets.

Tier 1 Capital: Core capital including common equity, retained earnings, and disclosed reserves.
Tier 2 Capital: Supplementary capital including revaluation reserves, hybrid instruments, and subordinated debt.

Stressed Assets: Include NPAs, restructured loans, and written-off assets.

=== SECTION 3: LOANS & LENDING ===

Types of Loans:
- Term Loans: Loans with fixed repayment schedule over a defined period.
- Working Capital Loans: Short-term loans to fund day-to-day operations.
- Overdraft: Credit facility allowing withdrawal beyond account balance up to a limit.
- Cash Credit: Similar to overdraft but specifically for businesses against stock/debtors.
- Letter of Credit (LC): Bank guarantee for trade transactions.
- Bank Guarantee (BG): Bank's commitment to pay if the customer defaults.

Home Loans: Secured loans against property. Typical LTV (Loan to Value) ratio is up to 80% for loans above Rs. 30 lakhs.

MCLR (Marginal Cost of Funds based Lending Rate): Benchmark rate introduced in 2016 for floating rate loans. Banks cannot lend below MCLR for most loans.

External Benchmark Lending Rate (EBLR): From October 2019, floating rate loans for retail and MSMEs must be linked to external benchmarks like RBI repo rate.

Loan Classification by Security:
- Secured Loans: Backed by collateral (home loans, auto loans, gold loans)
- Unsecured Loans: No collateral (personal loans, credit cards)

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR): Measures ability to service debt. DSCR = Net Operating Income / Total Debt Service. DSCR above 1.25 is generally acceptable.

Loan Restructuring: Process of modifying loan terms when borrower faces difficulty. Options include reducing interest rate, extending tenure, or providing moratorium.

=== SECTION 4: KYC (KNOW YOUR CUSTOMER) ===

KYC is a mandatory process for banks and financial institutions to verify customer identity and assess risk.

KYC Components:
- Customer Identification Program (CIP): Collecting and verifying customer identity documents.
- Customer Due Diligence (CDD): Assessing the nature of the customer's business and transaction patterns.
- Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD): Applied to high-risk customers such as Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs).
- Ongoing Monitoring: Continuous monitoring of customer transactions.

KYC Documents for Individuals:
- Proof of Identity: Aadhaar, PAN Card, Passport, Voter ID, Driving License
- Proof of Address: Utility bills, bank statements, Aadhaar, Passport
- Photograph

KYC Documents for Companies:
- Certificate of Incorporation
- Memorandum and Articles of Association
- Board Resolution
- List of Directors and Beneficial Owners

Beneficial Owner: A person who ultimately owns or controls more than 25% of shares or voting rights in an entity.

Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs): Individuals who hold or have held prominent public positions. Banks must apply EDD for PEPs.

Risk Categorization: Customers are classified as Low, Medium, or High risk based on occupation, transaction patterns, and geography.

KYC Periodicity:
- High-risk customers: Every 2 years
- Medium-risk customers: Every 8 years
- Low-risk customers: Every 10 years

Video-based Customer Identification Process (V-CIP): RBI-approved digital KYC method using live video interaction.

Central KYC Registry (CKYC): Centralized repository of KYC records maintained by CERSAI. One-time KYC valid across financial institutions.

=== SECTION 5: AML (ANTI-MONEY LAUNDERING) ===

Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained funds appear legitimate.

Three Stages of Money Laundering:
1. Placement: Introducing illegal funds into the financial system (e.g., cash deposits).
2. Layering: Concealing the trail through complex transactions (wire transfers, shell companies).
3. Integration: Reintroducing funds into the economy as legitimate money.

Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA) 2002: India's primary AML legislation. Requires banks to maintain records and report suspicious transactions.

Financial Intelligence Unit - India (FIU-IND): Nodal agency for receiving and analyzing suspicious transaction reports.

Reporting Requirements:
- Cash Transaction Report (CTR): All cash transactions above Rs. 10 lakhs must be reported monthly.
- Suspicious Transaction Report (STR): Any transaction suspected of money laundering must be reported within 7 days.
- Non-Profit Organization Transaction Report (NTR): Transactions by NPOs above Rs. 10 lakhs.
- Cross Border Wire Transfer Report (CWTR): All cross-border transactions above Rs. 5 lakhs.

Red Flags for Suspicious Transactions:
- Unusual large cash deposits or withdrawals
- Multiple transactions just below reporting thresholds (structuring)
- Transactions inconsistent with customer's business profile
- Rapid movement of funds through multiple accounts
- Transactions involving high-risk jurisdictions

FATF (Financial Action Task Force): International body setting global AML/CFT standards. India is a FATF member.

CFT (Combating Financing of Terrorism): Related to AML; banks must screen customers against terror watchlists like UN Security Council sanctions list.

Hawala: Informal value transfer system used to move money outside formal banking, commonly associated with money laundering.

=== SECTION 6: BANKING PRODUCTS & SERVICES ===

Current Account: Non-interest bearing account for businesses. Allows unlimited transactions.
Savings Account: Interest-bearing account for individuals. RBI deregulated savings account interest rates in 2011.
Fixed Deposit (FD): Term deposit with fixed interest rate for fixed tenure. DICGC insures deposits up to Rs. 5 lakhs.
Recurring Deposit (RD): Fixed monthly deposit for a fixed period earning interest.

NEFT (National Electronic Funds Transfer): Batch-based fund transfer system. Available 24x7 since December 2019.
RTGS (Real Time Gross Settlement): Real-time fund transfer for high-value transactions (minimum Rs. 2 lakhs). Available 24x7.
IMPS (Immediate Payment Service): Instant fund transfer available 24x7. No minimum amount.
UPI (Unified Payments Interface): Real-time payment system by NPCI. Enables instant transfers using VPA.

SWIFT: Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication. Used for international fund transfers.

Demat Account: Electronic account to hold securities. Managed by NSDL or CDSL depositories.

=== SECTION 7: BASEL NORMS ===

Basel I (1988): Introduced minimum capital requirement of 8% of risk-weighted assets. Only covered credit risk.

Basel II (2004): Three pillars framework:
1. Minimum Capital Requirements (credit, market, operational risk)
2. Supervisory Review Process
3. Market Discipline (disclosure requirements)

Basel III (2010, post-financial crisis): Strengthened capital requirements:
- Minimum Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1): 4.5%
- Tier 1 Capital: 6%
- Total Capital: 8% (9% in India)
- Capital Conservation Buffer: 2.5%
- Countercyclical Buffer: 0-2.5%
- Leverage Ratio: Minimum 3%
- Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR): Banks must hold enough high-quality liquid assets to cover 30 days of net cash outflows.
- Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR): Long-term stable funding must cover long-term assets.

=== SECTION 8: FINANCIAL RATIOS & ANALYSIS ===

Net Interest Margin (NIM): (Interest Income - Interest Expense) / Average Earning Assets. Measures profitability of lending activities.

Return on Assets (ROA): Net Income / Total Assets. Measures how efficiently a bank uses its assets. Good ROA for Indian banks: above 1%.

Return on Equity (ROE): Net Income / Shareholders' Equity. Measures return to shareholders. Good ROE: above 12-15%.

Cost to Income Ratio: Operating Expenses / Net Income. Lower is better. Good ratio: below 50%.

Gross NPA Ratio: Gross NPAs / Gross Advances. Measures asset quality. Lower is better.

Net NPA Ratio: Net NPAs / Net Advances. After provisions. Good ratio: below 2%.

PCR (Provision Coverage Ratio): Provisions / Gross NPAs. Higher means better provisioned. RBI recommends above 70%.

CASA Ratio: Current + Savings Deposits / Total Deposits. Higher CASA means lower cost of funds.

Credit Deposit (CD) Ratio: Total Loans / Total Deposits. RBI monitors this; high ratio may indicate liquidity risk.

=== SECTION 9: DIGITAL BANKING, FRAUD RISK, AND CONSUMER PROTECTION ===

UPI Fraud and Unauthorized Transactions:
- Customers should report unauthorized UPI, card, or internet-banking transactions immediately to reduce potential liability.
- Banks are expected to provide reporting channels such as branch support, helplines, SMS, mobile apps, or email.
- A delay in reporting can increase customer exposure depending on the facts of the case and the bank's policy framework.
- Fraud review generally checks whether the compromise arose from customer negligence, third-party breach, or a system issue at the bank.

Customer Liability Principles:
- Zero liability may apply when the fraud is caused by contributory fraud, negligence, or a system deficiency on the bank side.
- Limited liability may apply when a third-party breach occurs and the customer reports the issue within the prescribed period.
- Higher liability risk arises when the customer shares credentials such as OTPs, PINs, or passwords or delays reporting significantly.

Dormant and Inoperative Accounts:
- Savings and current accounts with no customer-induced transactions for an extended period may be treated as inoperative.
- Banks usually require re-KYC or customer confirmation before fully reactivating dormant relationships.
- Dormancy controls are important because abandoned accounts can be targets for fraud or operational errors.

Nomination and Deposit Claims:
- Nomination helps banks release deposit balances more smoothly after the account holder's death.
- Nominees receive the funds as trustees for legal heirs unless succession law says otherwise.
- Joint account holding instructions such as "either or survivor" affect how banks handle settlement and access.

=== SECTION 10: DEMO-READY INDIA QUESTIONS ===

What documents are required for KYC of an individual in India?
- Typical proof of identity includes Aadhaar, PAN, passport, voter ID, or driving licence.
- Typical proof of address includes Aadhaar, passport, utility bills, or bank statements.
- Banks also collect a photograph and may request PAN for tax-linked products or higher-risk profiles.

How do CTR and STR reporting differ in India?
- CTR covers cash transactions above the reporting threshold and is rule-based.
- STR covers suspicious activity regardless of transaction size and is judgment-based.
- Structuring, unusual layering, high-risk geographies, or activity inconsistent with customer profile can trigger STR review.

What is V-CIP and why does it matter?
- Video-based Customer Identification Process (V-CIP) is an RBI-approved digital onboarding process.
- It allows remote identity verification using live video interaction and document checks.
- It helps banks scale onboarding while maintaining stronger audit trails than simple document upload alone.
BASEL III MINIMUM CAPITAL RATIOS (ALWAYS CHECK TOGETHER):
- CET1 Ratio minimum: 4.5%
- Tier 1 Capital Ratio minimum: 6.0%
- Total Capital Ratio minimum: 8.0%
- Leverage Ratio minimum: 3.0%
- Capital Conservation Buffer: 2.5% above minimums
- Countercyclical Buffer: 0% to 2.5% (jurisdiction dependent)

Basel III compliance quick rule:
- A bank is compliant only if all required ratios meet or exceed thresholds.
- If any one ratio is below minimum, classify as NON-COMPLIANT and report deficit per ratio.

SECTION: KYC AUDIT FAILURE CONSEQUENCES
------------------------------------------------------------
KYC AUDIT FAILURE — REGULATORY CONSEQUENCES:
Banks and financial institutions that fail KYC audits face
the following consequences from regulators:

Regulatory Actions:
- Formal enforcement action from primary regulator
  (OCC, Federal Reserve, FDIC in US; FCA in UK; RBI in India)
- Cease and desist orders restricting new account openings
- Mandatory Written Agreement with remediation timeline
- Consent Orders requiring specific corrective actions
- Matters Requiring Attention (MRAs) from examiners

Financial Penalties:
- Civil Money Penalties (CMPs): up to $1,000,000 per day
  per violation under BSA in the United States
- Disgorgement of profits from non-compliant accounts
- Notable examples:
  HSBC: $1.9 billion fine (2012) for AML/KYC failures
  Standard Chartered: $1.1 billion (2019) for AML failures
  Deutsche Bank: $630 million (2017) for AML failures
  Capital One: $390 million (2021) for BSA violations

Operational Consequences:
- Appointment of independent compliance monitor
  (bank pays monitor's fees, typically $1-5M per year)
- Restriction on new products, services, or market expansion
- Forced exit from certain customer segments or geographies
- Enhanced supervision with more frequent examinations
- Mandatory staffing increases in compliance department

Reputational Consequences:
- Public enforcement actions published by regulators
- Loss of correspondent banking relationships
- Customer and investor confidence damage
- Share price decline (typically 3-8% on announcement)

Criminal Consequences (willful violations):
- Individual officer prosecution under BSA
- Deferred Prosecution Agreements (DPAs)
- Bank charter revocation in extreme cases

KYC AUDIT FAILURE 48-HOUR ACTION PLAN:
Hours 0-2:
1. Engage external compliance counsel immediately
   (attorney-client privilege protects communications)
2. Issue litigation hold on ALL audit documentation
   (do not alter, delete, or "fix" any records)
3. Notify Board Audit Committee (fiduciary duty)
4. Identify scope: which customers, branches, accounts affected

Hours 2-8:
5. Assess regulatory notification obligation
   (OCC/Fed/FDIC may require prompt notification)
6. Freeze onboarding of new high-risk customers immediately
7. Assemble internal investigation team
8. Begin gap analysis: which specific KYC requirements failed

Hours 8-48:
9. Draft remediation plan with realistic timeline
10. Contact primary regulator proactively
    (voluntary disclosure typically reduces penalties 30-50%)
11. Assess civil money penalty exposure
12. Review D&O insurance policy for coverage
13. Prepare board and investor communications if material

Proactive regulator contact is the single most effective
way to reduce penalties in a KYC audit failure scenario.

SECTION: FRAUD DISCOVERY CRISIS PROTOCOL
------------------------------------------------------------
BANK FRAUD DISCOVERY — IMMEDIATE ACTION PROTOCOL:

Minute 0-30 (RIGHT NOW):
1. Call General Counsel immediately
   Attorney-client privilege protects everything said after this
2. Issue litigation hold immediately
   Preserve ALL trading records, emails, communications, system logs
   One deleted record = potential obstruction of justice charge
3. Do NOT alert the implicated trader(s) yet
   Risk of evidence destruction, flight risk, or self-harm
4. Lock trading system access for implicated desk
5. Secure physical access to trading floor

Hours 0-2:
6. Notify CEO and Board Audit Committee
   They have fiduciary duty — notify within 2 hours
7. Engage forensic accounting firm immediately
   You need independent verification of the fraud amount
8. Engage external criminal defense counsel
   Separate from regular counsel — specialized expertise needed
9. Assess whether fraud is ongoing — stop the bleeding
10. Do NOT make any public statements — not one word

Regulatory Notifications (time-sensitive):
11. SAR filing with FinCEN — MANDATORY under BSA
    Legally required — do not tip off subject before filing
    File as soon as practicable, within 30 days maximum
12. Notify primary federal regulator proactively
    OCC for national banks, Federal Reserve for state members,
    FDIC for state non-members
    Prompt notification = cooperation credit = reduced penalties
13. Assess SEC disclosure obligation
    If fraud is material (typically >5% of net income),
    8-K filing may be required within 4 business days
14. Check FINRA reporting if broker-dealer operations involved
15. Review D&O insurance policy immediately

Hours 8-48:
16. Full forensic audit of trading desk — minimum 12 months back
17. Identify all counterparties to fraudulent trades
18. Asset tracing — where did the money go?
19. Prepare board resolution authorizing investigation
20. Consider voluntary disclosure to all relevant regulators
    Reduces penalties by 30-50% in most cases

Every minute of delay increases criminal and civil exposure.
Proactive regulator contact is your single best legal defense.

SECTION: DIVORCE FINANCIAL PROTECTION
------------------------------------------------------------
DIVORCE WITH JOINT INVESTMENTS — WHAT TO KNOW:

Immediate Legal Actions:
- Do NOT move, liquidate, or transfer any joint assets
  without legal counsel — this constitutes dissipation
  and seriously harms your legal position
- Engage a divorce attorney immediately
- Request a financial restraining order if concerned
  about spouse moving or hiding assets
- All joint investments subject to equitable distribution
  (community property states: CA, TX, AZ, NV, WA, ID, LA, WI, NM)
  (equitable states: all others — fair but not necessarily 50/50)

Asset Protection Steps:
- Get full written inventory of ALL joint accounts immediately
- Do not close joint accounts unilaterally (legal risk)
- Change passwords on YOUR individual (non-joint) accounts only
- Update beneficiary designations on YOUR individual accounts
- Monitor joint accounts for unauthorized activity

FDIC Coverage in Divorce:
- Joint accounts: insured $500,000 total ($250,000 per co-owner)
- After legal separation: restructure based on new ownership
- Amounts over $500,000 in joint accounts are uninsured
- Example: $2,000,000 joint account = $1,500,000 UNINSURED

Retirement Account Division:
- 401(k), 403(b), pension plans require QDRO
  (Qualified Domestic Relations Order)
- QDRO allows division WITHOUT triggering early withdrawal penalty
- IRA accounts: transfer incident to divorce is tax-free
- Do NOT withdraw from retirement accounts — 10% penalty applies

Tax Implications:
- IRC Section 1041: transfers between spouses incident to divorce
  are generally income-tax-free
- Capital gains tax triggered when liquidating joint investments
- Basis carries over to receiving spouse
- Consult CPA before any liquidation of joint assets
- Short-term vs long-term capital gains treatment applies

Timing:
- Do NOT make major investment changes until legal division complete
- Court may freeze joint assets during proceedings
- Financial advisor engagement should follow legal separation agreement

SECTION: BASEL III ALL THRESHOLDS (SINGLE CHUNK)

BASEL III MINIMUM CAPITAL RATIOS — ALL REQUIREMENTS:

Capital Requirements (all four together):
CET1 (Common Equity Tier 1) Ratio: minimum 4.5%
Tier 1 Capital Ratio: minimum 6.0%
Total Capital Ratio: minimum 8.0%
Leverage Ratio: minimum 3.0%

Capital Buffers (additional requirements):
Capital Conservation Buffer: 2.5% (on top of minimums)
  Making effective minimums:
  CET1 effective minimum: 7.0% (4.5% + 2.5% buffer)
  Tier 1 effective minimum: 8.5% (6.0% + 2.5% buffer)
  Total Capital effective minimum: 10.5% (8.0% + 2.5% buffer)
Countercyclical Buffer: 0% to 2.5% (jurisdiction-set)
G-SIB Surcharge: 1.0% to 3.5% (for global systemically important banks)

Liquidity Requirements:
LCR (Liquidity Coverage Ratio): minimum 100%
  Must hold enough HQLA to survive 30-day stress scenario
NSFR (Net Stable Funding Ratio): minimum 100%
  Stable funding must exceed required stable funding

COMPLIANCE CHECK — HOW TO EVALUATE:
Step 1: Compare each ratio to its minimum
Step 2: Note any ratio below minimum as a VIOLATION
Step 3: Calculate deficit: actual% minus minimum%
Step 4: Overall verdict: COMPLIANT only if ALL ratios pass

EXAMPLE COMPLIANCE CHECK:
CET1: 4.6% vs 4.5% minimum → PASS (+0.1% margin)
Tier 1: 5.7% vs 6.0% minimum → FAIL (-0.3% deficit)
Total Capital: 7.8% vs 8.0% minimum → FAIL (-0.2% deficit)
Leverage: 2.9% vs 3.0% minimum → FAIL (-0.1% deficit)
VERDICT: NON-COMPLIANT — 3 of 4 ratios below minimum

RBI IMPLEMENTATION (India) — HIGHER MINIMUMS:
CET1: minimum 5.5% (higher than Basel III)
Tier 1: minimum 7.0% (higher than Basel III)
Total Capital: minimum 9.0% (higher than Basel III)
Leverage: minimum 3.5% for D-SIBs, 3.0% for others

SECTION: IMPOSSIBLE INVESTMENT REQUESTS
------------------------------------------------------------
IMPOSSIBLE INVESTMENT GUARANTEES — HOW TO RESPOND:

The following are IMPOSSIBLE in legitimate investing:
- Guaranteed returns above 5% with zero risk
- Doubling money in short timeframes (6-12 months) safely
- Any investment with "no risk" and high returns
- "Safe" investments returning 15%+ consistently

Reality of risk-return tradeoff:
- Zero-risk instruments (current rates ~2024-2025):
  Treasury Bills (3-6 month): ~4.8-5.3%
  High-Yield Savings Accounts: ~4.5-5.0%
  Money Market Funds: ~4.8-5.1%
  6-Month CDs: ~4.5-5.0%

- Higher return = higher risk (always):
  S&P 500 historical average: ~10% annually (with volatility)
  Small-cap stocks: ~12% average (higher volatility)
  Emerging markets: ~8-11% average (high volatility)
  Venture capital: ~20-25% average (with frequent total losses)

Required return calculations:
- $200K → $1M in 2 years: requires 123.6% annually (impossible)
- $50K → $500K in 10 years: requires 25.9% annually (very high risk)
- $100K → $1M in 20 years: requires 12.2% annually (aggressive but possible)
- $100K → $1M in 30 years: requires 7.9% annually (moderate, realistic)

When asked for impossible guarantees:
1. Refuse clearly and immediately
2. Explain the risk-return tradeoff
3. State what zero-risk instruments actually return
4. Offer realistic educational alternatives
5. Never ask for more information to "fulfill" the request
